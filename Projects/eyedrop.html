Putting eye drops in your eye might seem simple, but it’s surprisingly easy to get wrong — you blink too soon, miss the target, or the liquid just drains away. We looked at how different kinds of liquids (some watery, some thick), how fast they hit the eye, and the state of your eye—dry or wet (tear film)—all affect the spread of the drug droplet on the eyelike surface.

Drops on wetted eye replicas spread more and cover more of the eye, especially at higher impact speeds. The drug drops merge with the tear film (miscible) and spread farther till the representative sclera in eyeball replicas. On dry eyes, the liquid drops didn’t spread as far and stopped sooner. But interestingly, once you get to the moment just before a person blinks (about 100 milliseconds after impact), how far the liquid spreads depends more on how wet the eye is than how fast the drop was moving.

We also tested firing tiny liquid jets generated using sparks. These jets can shoot out at very high speeds and in small volumes, much like a precise squirt. On wet eyes, they spread well and looked neat; on dry eyes, they sometimes splashed or bounced off, especially when they were thinner and faster. It turns out that thicker jets spread less but are also less messy, and there’s a sweet spot for getting the jet to land well without causing splatter.

Using scaling laws, we also showed how dimensionless numbers capture the spreading of droplets of different fluids on an eyeball replica.

All in all, we found that getting eye drops to work better isn’t just about what’s in the bottle — it’s also about how it hits the eye and whether the eye is already moist. Our work lays the foundation for smarter eye drop tools and techniques that waste less medication and make eye treatments more effective, especially for people with dry eyes or using viscous drug solutions.

Idera Lawal*, Pankaj Rohilla*, Eliana Rodriguez, Phuong Pham, and Jeremy Marston

Delivery of viscous drops and jets to eyeball replicas, International Journal of Pharmaceutics, 2025.
